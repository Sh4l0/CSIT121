Speech Recognition Grammar Specification is a W3C web standard. More specifically, it defines syntax for rules put on spoken inputs by a user. Overall, providing a consolidated way to measure and categorize words spoken by a user. On top of this, it allows the developer to specify how specific words or grammars should be interpreted by their speech engine.


Grammars are sets of patterns of words that tell an SRS(Speech recognition System) what a user might say. So essentially, this standard allows developers to specify which words and phrases they want the SRS to understand/recognize. The syntax for grammars has two forms: Augmented BNF (ABNF) and XML. Each form provides its own respective benefits and downsides, depending on usage.


This standard has several iterations, but I will focus on version 1.0. Its scope is stated as “to permit a speech application to indicate to a recognizer what it should listen for, specifically: Words that may be spoken, Patterns in which those words may occur, Spoken language of each word.” It is currently copyrighted by MIT, ERCIM, and Keio.
